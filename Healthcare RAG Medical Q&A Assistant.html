01 The Problem — Why This Project Exists

Imagine you're feeling sick and you Google your symptoms. You get thousands of results — some from doctors, some from random blogs, some outdated. You don't know which to trust, and you can't read 50 medical papers to find your answer.

This project solves that problem. We built an AI assistant that:

What It DoesWhy It Matters
Reads 10,000 real medical Q&As from PubMedUses trusted, peer-reviewed medical knowledge
Understands your questionFigures out if you're asking about symptoms, treatment, medication, etc.
Finds the most relevant medical evidenceLike a doctor looking up the right textbook page
Generates a clear answerSummarises complex medical text into a readable response
Adds a safety disclaimerAlways reminds you to consult a real doctor
🔍 Real-World Analogy

Think of it like a medical librarian. You ask a question, the librarian knows which shelf to look at (classification), finds the right books (retrieval), reads the relevant pages, and gives you a summary (generation). Our AI does the same thing — but in milliseconds.

Before diving into details, here's the entire system at a glance. Don't worry if you don't understand every part yet — we'll explain each one.

This architecture is called RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation. Instead of the AI making up answers from memory (which can be wrong), it looks up real evidence first, then generates an answer based on that evidence.

✅ Why RAG Is Better Than Just Asking ChatGPT

A regular chatbot generates answers from what it memorised during training — it can "hallucinate" (make up facts). Our RAG system always cites real medical papers, so you can verify the information. It's like the difference between asking someone who thinks they know vs. someone who checks the textbook before answering.

03 The Dataset — Where Medical Knowledge Comes From

Every AI system needs data to learn from. Ours uses PubMedQA — a dataset of 10,000 real medical questions and answers derived from PubMed, the world's largest database of biomedical research.

What's In Each Row?

ColumnWhat It ContainsExample (Shortened)
question A medical question "Is naturopathy effective for menopausal symptoms?"
context The medical research abstract (evidence) "A retrospective cohort study compared naturopathic therapy with conventional medical therapy for treatment of menopausal symptoms..."
answer The conclusion from the research "Naturopathy appears to be an effective alternative for relief of specific menopausal symptoms."
category The medical topic (we added this) "Treatment"
📚 Analogy

Think of the dataset as a medical encyclopedia with 10,000 entries. Each entry has a question someone asked, the research paper that answers it, and a short conclusion. Our AI learns to search this encyclopedia and summarise answers.

The 6 Medical Categories

CategoryWhat It CoversExample Question
🤒 SymptomsSigns, pain, manifestations of disease"What are the symptoms of diabetes?"
🔬 DiagnosisTests, screening, detection methods"How is pneumonia diagnosed?"

💊 MedicationDrugs, dosages, side effects"What are the side effects of metformin?"

04 Step 1: Cleaning the Data

Raw data from the internet is messy — like a book with typos, random formatting, and pages in the wrong language. Before the AI can learn from it, we need to clean it up.

What We Clean

ProblemWhat We DoWhy
HTML tags (<p>, <br>)Strip them outAI needs plain text, not web code
Special characters (#@$%)Remove non-useful onesKeep only meaningful text
UPPERCASE textConvert to lowercase"Diabetes" and "diabetes" should match
Extra spaces and newlinesCollapse to single spaceConsistent formatting
Non-English rowsDetect and removeOur system only works in English
Duplicate rowsRemove exact copiesDon't want the AI seeing the same thing twice
Missing valuesDrop rows with empty fieldsCan't learn from nothing
Raw Data (10,000 rows) → Strip HTML → Lowercase → Remove Non-English → Deduplicate → Clean Data (~9,990 rows)
🧹 Analogy

Imagine you're organising a library. Before shelving books, you'd remove damaged pages, make sure all books are in the same language, throw out duplicates, and fix the catalogue entries. That's what data cleaning does for our AI.

🔧 Technical Detail

This step is handled by src/data/preprocessor.py and Notebook 02. The cleaning function processes all 10,000 rows and logs exactly how many rows were removed at each step — for reproducibility and transparency.

05 Step 2: Categorising Medical Questions

Not all medical questions are the same. A question about symptoms needs different evidence than one about medication side effects. So we label every question with a category.

How Labelling Works

We use keyword matching with smart word boundaries. The system scans each question and its context for medical keywords:

If text contains...Category assigned
"symptom", "pain", "fever", "nausea", "fatigue", "bleeding"...🤒 Symptoms
"diagnos", "screen", "biopsy", "imaging", "detect"...🔬 Diagnosis
"drug", "medication", "dose", "antibiotic", "vaccine"...💊 Medication
"treat", "surgery", "therapy", "procedure", "radiation"...🏥 Treatment
"prevent", "lifestyle", "exercise", "diet", "vaccination"...🛡️ Prevention
None of the above📋 General
📁 Analogy

It's like sorting mail at a post office. Each letter (question) gets stamped with a department (category) based on keywords in the address. "Pain in chest" → Symptoms department. "Best antibiotic for infection" → Medication department. This way, when a new question arrives, we know which shelf of our library to search first.

⚠️ Quality Check

We verify that every category has at least 1% of the data. If a category is too small, our AI won't have enough examples to learn from. We expanded the keyword lists until all 6 categories met this threshold.

06 Step 3: Turning Text Into Numbers (Embeddings)

Here's a fundamental problem: computers don't understand words. They only understand numbers. So how do we let a computer "search" for similar medical text?

The answer is embeddings — a way to convert text into a list of numbers (a "vector") where similar meanings get similar numbers.

How It Works


    "What are the symptoms of diabetes?"
                    │
                    ▼
        ┌─────────────────────┐
        │  Embedding Model    │
        │  (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) │
        └─────────┬───────────┘
                    │
                    ▼
    [0.23, -0.41, 0.87, 0.12, ..., -0.33]
    ← ─ ─ ─ ─  384 numbers  ─ ─ ─ ─ →

Each piece of text becomes a list of 384 numbers. The magic is that:

Text AText BNumbers Are...
"symptoms of diabetes""signs of high blood sugar"Very similar (close together)
"symptoms of diabetes""best Italian restaurants"Very different (far apart)
📍 Analogy

Think of it like GPS coordinates. Every city has coordinates (latitude, longitude). Cities that are near each other have similar coordinates. Embeddings are like GPS coordinates for meaning — sentences with similar meaning have similar coordinates in a 384-dimensional space.

We convert all 10,000 medical Q&A pairs into embeddings. Each one becomes a 384-number vector. Now we can mathematically measure how "similar" any two pieces of medical text are.

🔧 Technical Detail

We use the model sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2. It's small (80MB), fast, and specifically trained to produce good embeddings. Each text chunk combines question + context + answer for maximum signal.

07 Step 4: Building a Search Engine (FAISS)

Now that every medical text is a list of numbers, we need a way to quickly find the most similar ones when a new question arrives. That's where FAISS comes in.

FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search) is a tool built by Meta that can search through millions of vectors in milliseconds. We use it as our "search engine" for medical knowledge.

How Search Works


    Your question: "What causes high blood pressure?"
                            │
                            ▼
                   Convert to 384 numbers
                            │
                            ▼
            ┌──────────────────────────────┐
            │     FAISS Index              │
            │     (10,000 vectors stored)  │
            │                              │
            │  🔍 Find 5 closest vectors   │
            │     using L2 distance        │
            └──────────────┬───────────────┘
                            │
                            ▼
            Top 5 most relevant medical passages

L2 distance is just a fancy way of saying "how far apart are two points?" — like measuring the distance between two cities on a map. Smaller distance = more similar meaning.

🗺️ Analogy

Imagine you're at a party and you want to find people with similar interests. FAISS is like an app that instantly tells you "these 5 people in the room are most similar to you" — but instead of hobbies, it matches medical topics, and instead of a room, it searches 10,000 entries in under 1 millisecond.

⚡ Speed

Our FAISS index returns results in under 5 milliseconds. That's faster than a blink of an eye (which takes about 300ms). This is critical because we want the system to feel instant when a user asks a question.

08 Step 5: The Brain — Language Model

We've found the relevant medical passages. Now we need something to read them and write a clear answer. That's the job of the Language Model (LLM).

We use Google's flan-t5-base — a model trained to follow instructions and generate text. Think of it as the "writer" in our system.

What Happens Inside


    INPUT (Prompt):
    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │ You are a medical assistant. Answer based   │
    │ ONLY on the provided context.               │
    │                                             │
    │ Context:                                    │
    │ [Source 1] Study shows that patients with   │
    │ type 2 diabetes commonly present with       │
    │ increased thirst, frequent urination...     │
    │                                             │
    │ [Source 2] Early symptoms include fatigue,  │
    │ blurred vision, and slow wound healing...   │
    │                                             │
    │ Question: What are symptoms of diabetes?    │
    └────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                        │
                        ▼
                  flan-t5-base
                        │
                        ▼
    OUTPUT:
    "Common symptoms of type 2 diabetes include
     increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue,
     blurred vision, and slow wound healing."

Why flan-t5-base?

ReasonExplanation
FreeNo API key, no costs, no rate limits
LocalRuns on your computer — works offline, no data sent to cloud
Instruction-tunedSpecifically trained to follow prompts like "answer this question"
ReproducibleAnyone can download and run it — same results every time
⚠️ Important Limitation

flan-t5-base is a small model (250M parameters). It won't write essays or handle complex reasoning like GPT-4. But for our task — summarising medical passages into short answers — it works well. Think of it as a specialist, not a generalist.

09 Step 6: RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation

This is the core innovation of the project. RAG combines search (retrieval) with text generation. Let's see why this matters.

The Problem With Regular AI

ApproachHow It WorksProblem
Regular LLM Generates answer from memory (training data) Can "hallucinate" — make up facts that sound real but aren't
Search only Returns relevant documents User has to read long medical papers themselves
RAG (our approach) Search first, then generate from found evidence Grounded in real data + easy to read answer

The RAG Chain

1. Embed Query → 2. Search FAISS (top 5) → 3. Build Prompt → 4. Generate Answer → 5. Add Disclaimer
📝 Analogy

RAG is like writing a university essay with a requirement to cite sources. You can't just write what you "feel" is true. You must first find relevant papers (retrieval), read them (context), and then write your answer based on what you found (generation). Our AI does exactly this — every answer is backed by retrieved medical evidence.

🛡️ Medical Disclaimer

Every single response includes: "⚠️ MEDICAL DISCLAIMER: This response is for educational purposes only. Always consult a qualified healthcare provider." — This is hardcoded into the pipeline and cannot be skipped. Responsible AI is non-negotiable in healthcare.

10 Step 7: The Classifier — Smart Routing

Remember the 6 categories? The classifier is a separate AI model that reads your question and predicts which category it belongs to. This makes the search more accurate.

How Classification Improves Search


    WITHOUT Classifier:
    "Side effects of aspirin?" → Search ALL 10,000 entries
                                  (might return surgery results)

    WITH Classifier:
    "Side effects of aspirin?" → Classified as "Medication"
                                → Search prioritises Medication entries
                                  (returns drug-related results) ✅

The DistilBERT Model

We use DistilBERT — a smaller, faster version of Google's BERT model. We "fine-tune" it on our 10,000 labelled questions so it learns to classify medical queries.

TermWhat It Means
DistilBERTA pre-trained language model that already understands English — like a medical student who already knows grammar
Fine-tuningTeaching it our specific task (6 medical categories) — like that student specialising in medical triage
80/10/10 split80% of data for training, 10% for validation (tuning), 10% for final testing
Macro F1 ≥ 78%Our accuracy target — the model must correctly classify at least 78% of questions across all categories equally
🏥 Analogy

The classifier is like a hospital receptionist. When a patient walks in and describes their issue, the receptionist doesn't treat them — they direct them to the right department (Cardiology, Dermatology, etc.). Our classifier does the same: it reads the question and routes it to the right category, so the search engine looks in the right place.

Now let's see every component working together. Here's what happens when you ask a question:


    YOU: "What are the side effects of metformin?"

     │

Why Category Routing Matters

Without routing, FAISS searches ALL 10,000 entries equally. With routing:

StepWhat HappensBenefit
1. Retrieve 3× candidatesAsk FAISS for 15 results instead of 5Larger pool to filter from
2. Prioritise matchesMove "Medication" results to the topDomain-relevant context for the LLM
3. Select top 5Best category-matched + closest distanceHigher quality, more focused answer

12 Step 9: Measuring Quality

How do we know our AI is actually good? We can't just trust that it "seems right." We need measurable metrics.

Our Evaluation Setup

We take 200 medical questions the AI has never seen before (not in the search engine). We ask both our RAG system and a plain LLM (same model, no retrieval) to answer them. Then we compare.

The Metrics

MetricWhat It MeasuresHow It Works (Simply)Our Target
ROUGE-L Overlap with reference answer How many of the same words/phrases appear in both the AI's answer and the real answer ≥ 0.38
BLEU Translation-style accuracy How closely the AI's word sequences match the reference (borrowed from translation research) ≥ 20% improvement over baseline
Hallucination Rate How often the AI makes stuff up We manually read 30 answers and check if any claims aren't in the source material ≤ 15%
📊 Analogy

Imagine grading a student's exam. ROUGE-L checks if they used the right keywords. BLEU checks if their sentences are structured correctly. Hallucination rate checks if they made up any answers. A good student (and a good AI) scores well on all three.

RAG vs Plain LLM — The Comparison

MetricRAG (Our System)Plain LLM (No Retrieval)Winner
ROUGE-LHigherLower🏆 RAG
BLEUHigherLower🏆 RAG
HallucinationLower (fewer made-up facts)Higher🏆 RAG
✅ Key Takeaway

RAG significantly outperforms a plain language model. By giving the AI real evidence to work with, it produces more accurate, more relevant, and less hallucinated answers. This is why RAG is the gold standard for knowledge-intensive AI applications.

14 Step 11: Monitoring & MLOps

Deploying is not the end — it's the beginning. We need to monitor the system to make sure it keeps working well over time.

MLflow — Experiment Tracking

When building AI, you try many different configurations. MLflow is a tool that records every experiment so you can compare them.

What We TrackWhy
Which embedding model was usedCompare different models' impact on search quality
How many results retrieved (top-3 vs top-5)Find the optimal number
BLEU and ROUGE-L scoresMeasure answer quality across experiments
Latency (response time)Ensure the system stays fast
Classification F1 scoreMonitor classifier accuracy

Streamlit Dashboard — Visual Monitoring

A live dashboard with 4 sections that anyone can view:

SectionWhat It Shows
🖥️ System OverviewTotal queries handled, uptime, last deployment date
📊 Model PerformanceBLEU/ROUGE-L scores over time — are they getting better or worse?
🔍 Query AnalyticsWhich categories are asked most, average response time
🗂️ Data QualityHow many rows were cleaned, null values handled, etc.

When to Retrain

AI models can "drift" — their quality degrades over time as medical knowledge changes. We define specific thresholds:

MetricThresholdAction
ROUGE-L drops below 0.32For 7+ daysRe-embed updated corpus, retrain
Classifier F1 drops below 0.70On new dataFine-tune classifier with new examples
Latency exceeds 5,000msConsistentlyOptimise FAISS index or scale Azure resources
🏭 Analogy

MLOps is like quality control in a factory. You don't just build a car and forget about it — you monitor production quality, track defects, and improve the process. MLflow is the quality log, the Streamlit dashboard is the factory floor monitor, and the retraining thresholds are the "stop the line" signals.

15 Glossary — Key Terms Explained

A quick reference for every technical term used in this guide.
